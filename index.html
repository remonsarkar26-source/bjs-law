Supreme Law of the Republic
The Constitution of Bangladesh
The foundational legal framework specifying the structure of governance, checks and balances, and guaranteed fundamental human rights.
Procedural Law
Code of Civil Procedure, 1908
The procedural backbone governing the administration, trial stages, and enforcement of decrees in civil disputes in Bangladesh.

1. Res Judicata vs. Res Sub-Judice

These two pillars ensure the finality of litigation and prevent a single cause of action from creating concurrent conflicting decisions or infinite trials between identical parties.

Legal Parameter Res Sub-Judice (Section 10) Res Judicata (Section 11)
Core Meaning A matter currently pending active trial in a competent court. A matter that has already been conclusively decided by a competent court.
Court's Action The court **stays the trial** of any subsequent suit until the first matter finishes. The court **completely bars the institution** or trial of the subsequent suit out of hand.
Prerequisites Two suits filed concurrently; the matter in issue must be directly and substantially identical in both. A previously decided suit exists; the matter was directly and substantially heard and finally decided.
Objective To prevent courts of concurrent jurisdiction from rendering conflicting decisions on the same right. To enforce the maxims of finality and prevent a defendant from being vexed twice for the same cause.

2. Temporary Injunctions: Order 39 (Rules 1-2)

Temporary injunctions are an equitable relief mechanism intended to preserve the exact physical status quo of a disputed property pending the final disposal of the suit.

The Golden Tripartite Test: To secure protection under Order 39, the plaintiff must prove: 1. Prima Facie Case: A bonafide, triable issue exists. 2. Irreparable Loss: Injury that cannot be adequately compensated by financial damages. 3. Balance of Inconvenience: Greater hardship will fall on the plaintiff if the injunction is refused than on the defendant if granted.
Order 39 R 1 Cases Where Temporary Injunction May Be Granted ▶

Granted where it is proved by affidavit or otherwise that any property in dispute in a suit is in danger of being wasted, damaged, or alienated by any party, or wrongfully sold in execution of a decree, or where the defendant threatens to remove or dispose of their property to defraud creditors.

Order 39 R 2 Injunction to Restrain Breach of Contract or Injury ▶

In any suit for restraining the defendant from committing a breach of contract or other injury, the plaintiff may apply for a temporary injunction to restrain the defendant from committing the breach or injury complained of, or any breach of contract or injury of a like kind arising out of the same contract.

3. Rejection of Plaint (O. 7 R. 11) vs. Return of Plaint (O. 7 R. 10)

When a plaint suffers from structural defects, the Code provides distinct pathways based on whether the defect is a fatal legal bar or a simple choice of the wrong forum.

O. 7 R. 11 Rejection of Plaint (Fatal Faults) ▶

The court **must reject** the plaint under four explicit statutory conditions:

  • A
    Where it does not disclose a clear cause of action.
  • B
    Where the relief claimed is undervalued, and the plaintiff fails to correct it within the time fixed by the court.
  • C
    Where the relief is properly valued but written on insufficiently stamped paper, and the default is not cured.
  • D
    Where the suit appears from the statement in the plaint to be completely barred by any law.
Crucial Decree Impact: A rejection of a plaint under Order 7 Rule 11 is deemed a "Decree" under Section 2(2) CPC. Therefore, it is appealable and does not bar the filing of a fresh plaint on the same cause of action.
O. 7 R. 10 Return of Plaint (Wrong Forum) ▶

The plaint is returned at any stage of the suit to be presented to the proper Court in which the suit should have been instituted. This occurs exclusively due to a lack of pecuniary, territorial, or subject-matter jurisdiction. The suit is not killed; it is simply redirected to the correct court.

Substantive Equity Law
The Specific Relief Act, 1877
Statutory framework providing tailored, equitable remedies such as recovery of possession, specific performance, rescission, cancellation, and injunctions.

1. Recovery of Possession: Section 8 vs. Section 9

The Act provides two distinct civil options for individuals seeking recovery of immovable property depending on title strength and the urgency of the dispossession.

Parameter Section 8 (Title-Based) Section 9 (Possession-Based Summary Suit)
Core Foundation Based entirely on regular legal ownership or title. Based strictly on previous peaceful possession; title is irrelevant.
Limitation Period **12 Years** under Article 142 or 144 of the Limitation Act. **6 Months** from the exact date of dispossession.
Requirement Plaintiff must prove dynamic ownership and superior title. Must prove sudden, dispossession without consent and outside due process of law.
Remedy Against State A regular suit under Section 8 **can** be filed against the Government. Section 9 explicitly states **no suit can be brought** against the Government.
Appeal / Review Rules Regular decree layout: **Appeals and Reviews are allowed**. **No Appeal or Review lies** from any order or decree passed under Section 9. Revision is allowed.

2. Specific Performance: Section 12 vs. Section 21

Courts balance equity to decide when a party must complete a physical contract versus when financial damages provide a complete, adequate alternative.

Section 12 Contracts Which Can Be Specifically Enforced ▶

Specific performance may be granted at judicial discretion under the following conditions:

  • A
    When the act agreed to be done is in the performance, wholly or partly, of a trust.
  • B
    When there exists no standard for ascertaining the actual damage caused by the non-performance of the act.
  • C
    When the act is such that pecuniary compensation would not afford adequate relief (e.g., transfers of unique immovable property).
Section 21 Contracts Which Cannot Be Specifically Enforced ▶

The court cannot command performance for the following statutory exclusions:

  • 1
    A contract where monetary compensation is an adequate relief option.
  • 2
    Contracts running into minute or numerous details, or dependent on personal qualifications/volition (e.g., marriage, personal services).
  • 3
    Contracts which are by nature highly determinable (can be terminated at will by either party).

3. Rescission of Contracts (Sec. 35) vs. Cancellation of Instruments (Sec. 39)

These remedies dissolve dynamic obligations or destroy physical legal documents to prevent future fraud or injustice.

Section 35 (Rescission of Contracts): Any person interested in a contract may sue to have it rescinded if the contract is voidable or terminable by the plaintiff, or if the contract is unlawful for causes not apparent on its face and the defendant is more to blame.
Section 39 (Cancellation of Instruments): Any person against whom a written instrument is void or voidable, and who has reasonable apprehension that such instrument, if left outstanding, may cause them serious injury, may sue to have it adjudged void or voidable and ordered to be delivered up and cancelled.

4. Types of Injunctions: Perpetual (Sec. 54) vs. Mandatory (Sec. 55)

Preventive relief is granted through injunctions that either restrain a wrongful act or compel the performance of a necessary task.

Sec 54 Perpetual Injunctions (Preventive Restraint) ▶

A perpetual injunction can only be granted by a final decree made at the hearing upon the merits of the suit. It permanently enjoys the defendant from asserting a right or committing an act which would be contrary to the rights of the plaintiff. It is triggered when the defendant invades or threatens to invade the plaintiff's right to, or enjoyment of, property.

Sec 55 Mandatory Injunctions (Compelled Performance) ▶

When, to prevent the breach of an obligation, it is necessary to compel the performance of certain acts which the Court is capable of enforcing, the Court may in its discretion grant an injunction to prevent the breach complained of, and also to compel performance of the requisite acts (e.g., ordering the demolition of a wall built illegally blocking easement rights).

Substantive Commercial Law
The Contract Act, 1872
The legal architecture establishing how agreements are structured, validated, performed, breached, and compensated.

1. Essential Elements of a Valid Contract: Section 10

An agreement remains a non-binding promise unless it satisfies the core validity criteria explicitly set forth under Section 10 of the Act.

Statutory Command: All agreements are contracts if they are made by the **free consent** of parties **competent to contract**, for a **lawful consideration** and with a **lawful object**, and are not hereby expressly declared to be void.
  • 1
    Competency (Section 11): Parties must be of the age of majority, of sound mind, and not disqualified by law.
  • 2
    Free Consent (Section 14): Consent is free when it is not caused by Coercion (Sec. 15), Undue Influence (Sec. 16), Fraud (Sec. 17), Misrepresentation (Sec. 18), or Mistake (Sec. 20-22).
  • 3
    Lawful Object & Consideration (Section 23): Must not be fraudulent, cause injury to others, or be opposed to public policy.

2. Structural Status: Void Agreements vs. Voidable Contracts

The Act draws sharp technical distinctions between agreements that carry zero legal life from inception and those that remain conditional on a party's choice.

Parameter Void Agreement: Section 2(g) Voidable Contract: Section 2(i)
Statutory Definition An agreement not enforceable by law is said to be void. An agreement enforceable by law at the option of one or more parties, but not at the option of the other.
Legal Status *Void ab initio* — Dead from the very beginning. Holds no legal existence. Valid and operational until explicitly repudiated by the aggrieved party.
Primary Causes Incompetency of parties (minority), unlawful objects, or agreements in restraint of marriage/trade. When consent to an agreement is obtained via coercion, undue influence, fraud, or misrepresentation.
Restitution Any benefit received must be restored under Section 65. If rescinded, benefits received must be returned to the other party under Section 64.

3. Special Modalities: Contingent Contracts vs. Quasi-Contracts

Contracts can be anchored to uncertain future occurrences or constructed implicitly by equity to avoid unjust enrichment.

Sec 31 Contingent Contracts (Conditional Obligations) ▶

A contingent contract is a contract to do or not to do something, if some event, collateral to such contract, does or does not happen. Performance depends completely on the arrival or failure of a third-party event (e.g., insurance contracts).

Sec 68-72 Quasi-Contracts (Relations Resembling Contracts) ▶

These are obligations imposed by law even in the absence of a formal agreement, founded on the equitable principle that no individual should grow rich off another's loss:

  • Section 68: Claim for necessaries supplied to a person incapable of contracting.
  • Section 69: Reimbursement of a person paying money due by another, in the payment of which he is interested.
  • Section 70: Obligation of a person enjoying the benefit of a non-gratuitous act.
  • Section 71: Responsibility of a finder of goods to take care of them and locate the owner.
  • Section 72: Liability of a person to whom money is paid or things delivered by mistake or under coercion.

4. Consequences of Breach of Contract: Section 73

When a party breaks an obligation, the law steps in to calculate financial remedies aimed at shifting the financial burden back to the defaulting party.

Section 73 (Compensation for Loss or Damage): When a contract has been broken, the party who suffers by such breach is entitled to receive from the party who has broken the contract, compensation for any loss or damage caused to him thereby, which **naturally arose in the usual course of things** from such breach, or which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from the breach.
Liquidated Damages Framework: Unlike English Common Law, which draws a strict distinction between liquidated damages (genuine pre-estimates) and penalties (punitive sums), courts in Bangladesh applying Section 74 look at the named sum as the absolute maximum cap. The court will only award **reasonable compensation** up to, but not exceeding, the amount stipulated in the contract.
Procedural & Substantive Policy
The Limitation Act, 1908
Statutory rules regulating the time thresholds within which suits, appeals, and applications must be brought before judicial forums.

1. The Harsh Reality of Section 3: Absolute Mandatory Dismissal

Section 3 stands as the unyielding gatekeeper of legal finality. It acts independently of whether the defense is explicitly raised by the opposing party.

Statutory Command: Subject to the provisions contained in sections 4 to 25 inclusive, every suit instituted, appeal preferred, and application made after the period of limitation prescribed therefor by the first schedule **shall be dismissed**, although limitation has not been set up as a defense.
Exam Strategy Warning: Section 3 leaves zero judicial discretion to equity. Even if a claim is morally flawless and true, if the clock has run out, the court holds no jurisdiction to hear it. It affects the remedy, not the underlying right.

2. Condonation of Delay: Section 5

Section 5 acts as an equitable valve, allowing extensions of time if the applicant satisfies the court that they had **sufficient cause** for not preferring the action within the statutory window.

Scope Where can Section 5 be applied? ▶

Applies strictly to **Appeals** and **Applications** (such as review, revision, or leave to appeal applications). The court may condone the delay if the appellant shows sufficient cause (e.g., severe illness, imprisonment, or a bona fide mistake of counsel).

The Exception Bar Absolute Exclusion of Suits ▶

Section 5 does NOT apply to original Suits or execution proceedings under Order 21 CPC.

If an original civil suit is delayed by even a single day beyond the First Schedule time-frame, Section 5 cannot be used to salvage it. The delay in original suits can only be offset by dynamic statutory exemptions like Legal Disability (Section 6) or Fraud (Section 18).

3. Exceptions: Legal Disability (Sec. 6) & Effect of Fraud (Sec. 18)

The law pauses or resets the limitation clock when the plaintiff is structurally incapable of pursuing their legal remedy due to internal disability or external deception.

Sec 6 Legal Disability (Clock Extension) ▶

Where a person entitled to institute a suit or make an application for the execution of a decree is, at the time from which the period of limitation is to be reckoned, a **minor**, **insane**, or an **idiot**, they may institute the suit or make the application within the same period after the disability has ceased.

  • If a person is affected by two consecutive disabilities (e.g., minor and insane), the clock remains paused until both disabilities are fully lifted.
  • Does not apply to appeals or regular interlocutory applications.
Sec 18 Effect of Fraud (Resetting the Clock) ▶

Where a person having a right to institute a suit or make an application has been kept from knowledge of such right or title by means of fraud, or where any document necessary to establish such right has been fraudulently concealed, the limitation clock **restarts** only when the fraud first becomes known to the affected party.

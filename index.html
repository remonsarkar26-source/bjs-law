FIR and Investigation (§§ 154–176)

§ 154First Information Report (FIR) — Cognizable offences▶

Every information relating to the commission of a cognizable offence, if given orally to an officer in charge of a police station, shall be reduced to writing by him or under his direction, and be read over to the informant; and every such information, whether given in writing or reduced to writing as aforesaid, shall be signed by the person giving it, and the substance thereof shall be entered in a book kept for that purpose by such officer in the form prescribed by the Government, and a copy of the information as so recorded shall be given forthwith, free of cost, to the informant.

Essentials of a valid FIR: (1) Must relate to a cognizable offence, (2) If oral, must be reduced to writing by the officer, (3) Must be read over to the informant, (4) Signed by the informant, (5) Entered in the station diary, (6) A free copy must be given to the informant immediately.
📌 FIR is NOT substantive evidence — it cannot prove the facts stated in it. Its only use is to corroborate or contradict the informant's testimony in court (Evidence Act §157). The FIR sets the criminal law in motion for cognizable offences.
Key principle: The earliest version of events (FIR) carries significant weight. If the FIR omits important facts, the prosecution may face difficulty. Delay in filing FIR must be explained — unexplained delay weakens the prosecution case.
§ 155Non-cognizable offences — complaint to Magistrate▶

When information is given to an officer in charge of a police station of the commission within the limits of such station of a non-cognizable offence, he shall enter or cause to be entered the substance of such information in a book prescribed, and refer the informant to the Magistrate.

Non-cognizable offence procedure: Police enter it in the NCR (Non-Cognizable Register) and refer the complainant to the Magistrate. Police CANNOT investigate without written order from a Magistrate of the first class or higher (§155(2)). The police have no power to arrest without warrant in NC offences.
§ 156–157Police investigation of cognizable offences▶

§156: Any officer in charge of a police station may, without the order of a Magistrate, investigate any cognizable case. §157: If from information received or otherwise, an officer in charge of a police station has reason to suspect the commission of an offence, he shall forthwith send a report to a Magistrate empowered to take cognizance, and shall proceed in person to the spot to investigate the facts.

Investigation steps: (1) Receive information (FIR), (2) Send report to Magistrate (§157), (3) Proceed to scene, (4) Examine witnesses (§161), (5) Search (§165), (6) Arrest if necessary (§54), (7) Submit chargesheet/final report (§173).

§ 173Chargesheet / Police Report after investigation▶

Chargesheet (§173 report): Contains: names of parties, nature of information, names of persons who appear to be acquainted with facts, whether accused is arrested/in custody/on bail, whether accused is forwarded for trial or released on bail. If there is sufficient evidence: chargesheet sent. If insufficient: final report (seeking closure) sent.
📌 The chargesheet (also called "Charge" or "Dakhila") is the police's formal statement that there is sufficient evidence to prosecute. Without a chargesheet (or a Magistrate's complaint under §190), no trial can begin.

Arrest (§§ 46–67)

§ 46How arrest is to be made▶

In making an arrest the police officer or other person making the same shall actually touch or confine the body of the person to be arrested, unless there be a submission to the custody by word or action. If such person forcibly resists the endeavour to arrest him, or attempts to evade the arrest, such police officer or other person may use all means necessary to effect the arrest.

Arrest requires: Physical touching/confinement OR submission to custody. A verbal declaration "You are arrested" without physical touching is NOT arrest unless the person submits. Police may use necessary force if the person resists — but CANNOT cause death to arrest a person accused of non-capital offence (§46(3)).
§ 54Arrest without warrant by police — cognizable cases▶

Any police officer may without an order from a Magistrate and without a warrant, arrest any person — (a) who has been concerned in any cognizable offence; (b) against whom a reasonable complaint has been made, or credible information has been received, or a reasonable suspicion exists, of his having been so concerned; (c) who has in his possession without lawful excuse any implement of house-breaking; (d) who has been proclaimed as an offender; (e) in whose possession anything is found which may reasonably be suspected to be stolen property; (f) who obstructs a police officer while in the execution of his duty, or who has escaped from lawful custody; (g) who is reasonably suspected of being a deserter from any armed forces; (h) who has been concerned in, or against whom a reasonable complaint has been made, of any act outside Bangladesh which, if committed in Bangladesh, would have been punishable under certain sections; (i) who, being a released convict, commits a breach of a rule made under CrPC.

Power to arrest without warrant: Very wide. "Reasonable suspicion" is the standard — not proof. Police can arrest on reasonable suspicion even without an FIR. However, the arrest must be justified and the person must be produced before a Magistrate within 24 hours (Article 33 Constitution).
📌 Constitutional right: Article 33 of the Constitution — a person arrested must be informed of grounds of arrest, must be allowed to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner, and must be produced before the nearest Magistrate within 24 hours (excluding travel time). Failure to comply = illegal detention = habeas corpus available (Article 102).
§ 61Person arrested to be brought before Magistrate within 24 hours▶

No police officer shall detain in custody a person arrested without warrant for a longer period than under all the circumstances of the case is reasonable, and such period shall not, in the absence of a special order of a Magistrate under section 167, exceed twenty-four hours exclusive of the time necessary for the journey from the place of arrest to the Magistrate's Court.

📌 24 hours rule: fundamental. After 24 hours, the police MUST produce the arrested person before a Magistrate. The Magistrate then decides — bail, remand, or release. If police hold beyond 24 hours without Magistrate's order, it is illegal detention.
§ 167Remand — custody beyond 24 hours▶

Whenever any person is arrested and detained in custody, and it appears that the investigation cannot be completed within the period of twenty-four hours fixed by section 61, and there are grounds for believing that the accusation or information is well-founded, the officer in charge shall transmit to the nearest Magistrate a copy of the entries in the diary and shall at the same time forward the accused to such Magistrate.

Remand (§167): If investigation is incomplete in 24 hours, police can get Magistrate's authorisation to detain further. The Magistrate may authorise custody (police remand or judicial custody) for up to 15 days at a time. Total remand for cognizable offence: 15 days police custody; further detention in judicial custody up to 60 days (non-capital) or 90 days (capital offences) — after which the accused must be released on bail if chargesheet not filed.
📌 Police remand vs Judicial custody: Police remand = accused held in police station for interrogation. Judicial custody = accused held in jail under court's authority. Courts are cautious about police remand — must show necessity for investigation.

Bail (§§ 496–502)

Bail is the temporary release of an accused from custody upon providing sureties to appear in court. Bail preserves the presumption of innocence. The right to bail depends on whether the offence is bailable or non-bailable.

§ 496Bail in bailable offences — it is a RIGHT▶

When any person other than a person accused of a non-bailable offence is arrested or detained without warrant by an officer in charge of a police station, or appears or is brought before a Court, and is prepared at any time while in the custody of such officer or at any stage of the proceedings before such Court to give bail, such person shall be released on bail.

Bailable offence — bail is a RIGHT, not discretion. The police officer OR court MUST release on bail. There is no discretion to refuse. The only issue is the amount and nature of security. However, if the accused cannot provide bail, he can be released on his own recognizance (§496 proviso).
§ 497Bail in non-bailable offences — it is DISCRETION▶

When any person accused of any non-bailable offence is arrested or detained without warrant, or appears or is brought before a Court, he may be released on bail, but he shall not be so released if there appear reasonable grounds for believing that he has been guilty of an offence punishable with death or imprisonment for life.

Three restrictions on bail in non-bailable offences: (1) Cannot be granted if there appear reasonable grounds he is guilty of an offence punishable with death or life imprisonment, (2) Under 16, women, and sick/infirm persons — may be released on bail even for serious offences (§497(1) proviso), (3) Bail can be cancelled if accused absconds, interferes with evidence, or commits another offence (§497(5)).
📌 Who can grant bail for non-bailable offences? Any Magistrate having jurisdiction to try the case, OR Sessions Court, OR High Court Division. For offences exclusively triable by Sessions Court — Magistrate can grant bail only at the investigation stage, not after commitment.

CrPC provides four distinct modes of trial, each suited to different levels of offence seriousness. The type of trial determines the procedure followed.

Sessions TrialProcedure for the most serious offences (§§265A–265L)▶
  • 1Commitment: Magistrate commits the case to Sessions Court after finding prima facie case
  • 2Opening case: Sessions Judge opens the case, prosecution presents facts
  • 3Charge: Sessions Judge frames charge (§265B); accused pleads guilty or not guilty
  • 4Prosecution evidence: All prosecution witnesses examined; accused can cross-examine
  • 5Defence: If case prima facie made out, accused called to enter defence; defence witnesses examined
  • 6Arguments: Both sides argue
  • 7Judgment: Conviction or acquittal. If death sentence — sent to HC for confirmation (§374)
Warrant Case TrialFor offences carrying more than 2 years (§§241A–250)▶

Two modes: (1) On police report (chargesheet) — §241A: Magistrate takes cognizance, hears accused, frames charge or discharges; (2) On Magistrate's complaint (§200): Magistrate examines complainant, may take cognizance. After charge is framed, prosecution evidence is led, accused enters defence, then judgment.

📌 In warrant case, unlike summons case, the charge is formally framed — a written document specifying the exact offence accused is charged with. This is important because the accused must know the specific allegation to prepare his defence.
Summary TrialExpeditious procedure for minor offences (§§260–265)▶

In a summary trial, the Magistrate proceeds as summarily as possible. No formal charge need be framed. The accused's plea, evidence, and finding are all noted briefly. Maximum imprisonment that can be imposed in a summary trial: 3 months.

Offences triable summarily (§260): Offences not punishable with death, life, or more than 2 years; theft where property value not exceeding Tk.200; receiving stolen property of value not exceeding Tk.200; dishonestly misappropriating property not exceeding Tk.200; certain other offences of a trivial nature.
§ 221–240Charge — framing and contents▶

§221: Every charge shall state the offence with which the accused is charged. If the law gives a specific name to the offence, the charge shall be in those words. §222: The charge shall contain such particulars as to the time and place and (in appropriate cases) the person against whom, or the thing in respect of which, the offence was committed, as are reasonably sufficient to give the accused notice of the matter with which he is charged.

Contents of a valid charge: (1) Name of offence (§221), (2) Particulars of offence — time, place, person, thing (§222), (3) Section of the law under which offence is punishable (§221), (4) Must be in writing, signed by the Magistrate/Judge. Errors in charge do not vitiate trial if accused is not misled (§535 — curing of irregularities).
📌 Doctrine of autrefois acquit/convict (§403): If a person has been once convicted or acquitted of an offence, he cannot be tried again for the same offence. This protects against double jeopardy (also see Constitution Art.35(3)).

Judgment and Sentencing

§ 366–373Judgment — form and content▶

§367: Every judgment in a criminal case shall be written in the language of the court, shall contain the point or points for determination, the decision thereon, and the reasons for the decision, and shall be dated and signed by the Judge.

Forms of judgment: (1) Acquittal — accused is found not guilty; (2) Conviction — accused is found guilty and sentence is pronounced. The judgment must contain points for determination, decision, and REASONS. A judgment without reasons is defective and can be set aside on appeal.
§ 374Death sentence — confirmation by High Court▶

When the Court of Session passes a sentence of death, the proceedings shall be submitted to the High Court Division, and the sentence shall not be executed unless it is confirmed by the High Court Division.

📌 Death sentence by Sessions Court does NOT automatically take effect — it MUST be confirmed by the High Court Division. This is a mandatory safeguard. The HC may confirm, commute, reduce, alter, or annul the sentence.

📌 No appeal from acquittal by the accused — acquittal means the accused is free. Government/complainant can appeal acquittals under §417. Appeal against conviction: the convicted person appeals. There is generally no second appeal in criminal cases (unlike CPC §100).
§ 435–439Revision — supervisory jurisdiction▶

§435: The High Court Division may call for and examine the record of any proceeding before any inferior criminal court for the purpose of satisfying itself as to the correctness, legality or propriety of any finding, sentence or order recorded or passed, and as to the regularity of any proceedings of such inferior court.

Revision vs Appeal: (1) Revision is supervisory — court looks at legality, correctness, propriety. Appeal is a re-hearing on merits. (2) In revision, the court does NOT reappreciate evidence as freely as in appeal. (3) Revision is available only when no appeal lies OR when the error is jurisdictional. (4) In revision, the court generally cannot enhance a sentence without notice to the accused.
📌 §561A — Inherent power of High Court: "Nothing in this Code shall be deemed to limit or affect the inherent power of the High Court to make such orders as may be necessary to give effect to any order under this Code, or to prevent abuse of the process of any Court or otherwise to secure the ends of justice." This is the residual power invoked when no specific provision applies.

Bangladesh · Law of Evidence
Evidence Act, 1872
Governs what evidence may be presented in court, how it must be proved, and the competency of witnesses. 167 sections in 3 Parts. Applies to all judicial proceedings.
167 Sections3 Parts🔴 BJS Highest PriorityAlso: Bar Council

Part I — Relevancy of Facts (Sections 1–55)

The Evidence Act 1872 determines what facts can be brought before a court and how they must be proved. It applies to all judicial proceedings in Bangladesh — civil and criminal. The Act has 3 Parts: Relevancy (§§1–55), Proof (§§56–100), and Production/Effect of Evidence (§§101–167).

§ 3Key Definitions — the most important in the Act▶

Court: Includes all judges and magistrates and all persons legally authorised to take evidence. Fact: means and includes (1) any thing, state of things, or relation of things, capable of being perceived by the senses; (2) any mental condition of which any person is conscious. Relevant: one fact is said to be relevant to another when the one is connected with the other in any of the ways referred to in the provisions of relevancy. Evidence: means and includes — (1) all statements which the Court permits or requires to be made before it by witnesses, in relation to matters of fact under inquiry — such statements are called oral evidence; (2) all documents produced for the inspection of the Court — such documents are called documentary evidence. Proved: A fact is said to be proved when, after considering the matters before it, the Court either believes it to exist, or considers its existence so probable that a prudent man ought, under the circumstances of the particular case, to act upon the supposition that it exists. Disproved: A fact is said to be disproved when the Court believes that it does not exist, or considers its non-existence so probable that a prudent man ought, under the circumstances of the particular case, to act upon the supposition that it does not exist. Not proved: A fact is said not to be proved when it is neither proved nor disproved.

📌Three states of fact: Proved, Disproved, and Not proved. 'Not proved' is the middle ground — this is crucial in criminal cases because the accused is entitled to acquittal if his guilt is 'not proved beyond reasonable doubt.'
§ 5Evidence may be given only of facts in issue and relevant facts▶

Evidence may be given in any suit or proceeding of the existence or non-existence of every fact in issue and of such other facts as are hereinafter declared to be relevant, and of no others.

📌Exclusionary rule: Evidence of irrelevant facts is NOT admissible. Only facts in issue and facts declared relevant by §§6–55 can be proved. This prevents courts being overwhelmed with irrelevant material.

§ 17–23Admissions▶

An admission is a statement, oral or documentary or contained in electronic form, which suggests any inference as to any fact in issue or relevant fact, and which is made by any of the persons, and under the circumstances, hereinafter mentioned.

Who can make admissions: (1) Party to the proceeding, (2) Agent of a party, (3) Party suing/sued in representative capacity, (4) Person whose position or liability is in issue, (5) Person from whom a party has derived his interest in the subject matter, (6) Any person referred to by the party.
📌Admission vs Confession: ALL confessions are admissions but NOT all admissions are confessions. Admission is broader — any statement suggesting an inference. Confession is specifically an admission of guilt in a criminal case. Admission can be made in civil or criminal cases.

§ 32Statements by persons who cannot be called as witnesses — Dying Declaration▶

Statements, written or verbal, of relevant facts made by a person who is dead, or who cannot be found, or who has become incapable of giving evidence, or whose attendance cannot be procured without an amount of delay or expense which under the circumstances of the case, appears to the Court unreasonable, are themselves relevant facts in the following cases — (1) When the statement is made by a person as to the cause of his death, or as to any of the circumstances of the transaction which resulted in his death, in cases in which the cause of that person's death comes into question.

Dying Declaration (§32(1)): (1) Statement by person as to CAUSE of his own death OR circumstances of transaction resulting in his death. (2) Admissible whether or not the person was under expectation of death at the time of making it (differs from English law which requires expectation of death). (3) The person must have since died — otherwise §32 does not apply; his statement would be given as oral evidence.
Illustration: A is fatally stabbed. While being taken to hospital, A tells the doctor: 'B stabbed me because I refused to sell my land.' A dies the next day. This dying declaration is admissible at B's murder trial under §32(1).
Case / Principle: Pakala Narayana Swami v Emperor (PC): A statement that pointed the finger at the accused as the probable cause of the declarant's death is admissible as a dying declaration even if the declarant does not know he is dying. The Bangladesh Evidence Act §32(1) requires no expectation of death.
📌Can a dying declaration alone convict? YES — if the court is satisfied that it is truthful and free from tutoring/infirmity. Corroboration is desirable but not legally required. The credibility and circumstances of making it are crucial.
§ 33Relevancy of certain evidence for proving truth of facts▶

Evidence given by a witness in a judicial proceeding, or before any person authorised by law to take it, is relevant for the purpose of proving, in a subsequent judicial proceeding, or in a later stage of the same judicial proceeding, the truth of the facts which it states, when the witness is dead or cannot be found, or is incapable of giving evidence, or is kept out of the way by the adverse party, or if his presence cannot be obtained without an amount of delay or expense.

📌§33 allows evidence from a previous proceeding to be used in a subsequent proceeding — but ONLY if the witness is now unavailable AND the adverse party had the opportunity to cross-examine in the previous proceeding.
§ 45Opinions of Experts▶

When the Court has to form an opinion upon a point of foreign law, or of science, or art, or as to identity of handwriting or finger impressions, the opinions upon that point of persons specially skilled in such foreign law, science or art, or in questions as to identity of handwriting or finger impressions, are relevant facts. Such persons are called experts.

Who is an expert? A person specially skilled in: (1) Foreign law, (2) Science or art (including medicine, engineering, ballistics, chemistry), (3) Identity of handwriting, (4) Finger impressions. Expert opinion is relevant — not conclusive. The court is not bound by expert opinion; it can accept or reject it.
📌Medical expert in murder trial: A forensic doctor's opinion on cause of death, time of death, and nature of injuries is expert opinion under §45. A ballistics expert's opinion on whether a particular gun fired the bullet is also expert opinion. The court evaluates the expert's qualifications, methodology, and reasoning.

Part II — On Proof (Sections 56–100)

This part deals with what needs to be proved, what does not need to be proved (judicial notice, admissions), and how documents must be proved.

§ 56–57Facts judicially noticed — need not be proved▶

§56: No fact of which the Court will take judicial notice need be proved. §57: The Court shall take judicial notice of the following facts — All laws in force in Bangladesh; All public Acts passed; Articles of War; Course of proceedings of Parliament; Territorial divisions of Bangladesh; Seals of courts; Accession/sign manuals of Presidents; All seals of public offices; Commencement/end and dates of Bangladesh; Official gazette contents; State of war or not; Names and titles of major government positions.

📌Judicial notice = court accepts a fact as established without requiring proof. Example: The court takes judicial notice that 1971 was the year of Bangladesh's independence. No evidence needed. Similarly, provisions of Bangladeshi law are judicially noticed.
§ 58Facts admitted need not be proved▶

No fact need be proved in any proceeding which the parties thereto or their agents agree to admit at the hearing, or which, before the hearing, they agree to admit by any writing under their hands, or which by any rule of pleading in force at the time they are deemed to have admitted by their pleadings.

📌In civil cases, facts admitted in pleadings need not be proved. In criminal cases, the accused can admit facts through his lawyer — but such admissions are construed strictly as the accused's liberty is at stake.

Burden of Proof

§ 101General burden of proof — on who asserts▶

Whoever desires any Court to give judgment as to any legal right or liability dependent on the existence of facts which he asserts, must prove that those facts exist. When a person is bound to prove the existence of any fact, it is said that the burden of proof lies on that person.

Golden rule: He who asserts must prove. Ei incumbit probatio qui dicit, non qui negat. In criminal cases: prosecution asserts guilt — prosecution must prove it beyond reasonable doubt.
📌Burden of proof has two aspects: (1) Legal/persuasive burden — overall obligation to prove your case (stays fixed throughout trial). (2) Evidential burden — duty to produce evidence at a particular stage (may shift). In criminal cases, legal burden is always on prosecution. Evidential burden may shift to accused when he relies on a special defence.
§ 102Burden of proof — on whom it lies▶

The burden of proof in a suit or proceeding lies on that person who would fail if no evidence at all were given on either side.

📌Test for burden of proof: If neither side produces any evidence — who loses? That person bears the burden of proof. In a civil suit for contract, if plaintiff produces nothing, plaintiff loses — so plaintiff bears the burden.
§ 103Burden of proof as to particular fact▶

The burden of proof as to any particular fact lies on that person who wishes the Court to believe in its existence, unless it is provided by any law that the proof of that fact shall lie on any particular person.

📌Specific burden: Even within a case, each specific fact must be proved by whoever wants the court to believe it. Example: Accused wants court to believe he was elsewhere at the time of crime — accused must prove the alibi (at least raise a reasonable doubt).
§ 105Burden of proof when accused relies on exception▶

When a person is accused of any offence, the burden of proving the existence of circumstances bringing the case within any of the General Exceptions in the Pakistan Penal Code, or within any special exception or proviso contained in any other part of the same Code, or in any law defining the offence, is upon him, and the Court shall presume the absence of such circumstances.

General Exceptions burden (§105): If accused claims private defence, unsound mind, intoxication, or any other general exception — the ACCUSED must prove it. The prosecution need not disprove it. Standard is preponderance of probability (balance of probabilities), not beyond reasonable doubt.
📌§105 is the counterpart to Penal Code §6 and General Exceptions. The presumption is against the accused — 'Court shall presume absence of such circumstances.' The accused must rebut this presumption by preponderance of probability.

Presumptions

§ 107–111Presumptions as to life and death▶

📌The 7-year presumption of death (§108): If a person has not been heard of for 7 years by persons who would naturally hear from him — the court may presume he is dead. The exact date of death is NOT presumed — only that death has occurred.

The fact that any person was born during the continuance of a valid marriage between his mother and any man, or within two hundred and eighty days after its dissolution, the mother remaining unmarried, shall be conclusive proof that he is the legitimate son of that man, unless it can be shown that the parties to the marriage had no access to each other at any time when he could have been begotten.

📌§112 is a conclusive presumption — it cannot be rebutted by DNA evidence alone (in traditional legal interpretation). Access means opportunity for sexual intercourse. If access is disproved, the presumption falls.

Witnesses — Competency, Examination, and Privilege

§ 118Who may testify — competency of witnesses▶

All persons shall be competent to testify unless the Court considers that they are prevented from understanding the questions put to them, or from giving rational answers to those questions, by tender years, extreme old age, disease, whether of body or mind, or any other cause of the same kind.

Competency vs Credibility: Competency = legal ability to testify (§118). Credibility = how much weight to give the testimony. A young child may be competent (can understand and answer) but have low credibility. A convicted criminal is competent but may lack credibility.
📌A child is competent to testify if the court is satisfied (usually after a preliminary examination) that the child understands the questions and can give rational answers. There is no minimum age. A lunatic can testify during a lucid interval.
§ 119Dumb witnesses▶

A witness who is unable to speak may give his evidence in any other manner in which he can make it intelligible, as by writing or by signs; and such evidence shall be deemed to be oral evidence.

📌A mute person can testify by writing or signs. Their testimony counts as oral evidence (not documentary). Important in cases involving witnesses with speech impairments.

Structure of CPC 1908

The Code of Civil Procedure 1908 governs the procedure for civil suits in Bangladesh. It has two parts: the Body (Sections 1–158 containing principles) and the Schedule (51 Orders with detailed rules). For BJS, you must know both.

§ 9Courts to try all civil suits unless barred▶

The Courts shall (subject to the provisions herein contained) have jurisdiction to try all suits of a civil nature excepting suits of which their cognizance is either expressly or impliedly barred.

Inherent civil jurisdiction: Civil courts can try all civil matters. To exclude jurisdiction, there must be express or clearly implied bar by statute. Courts interpret exclusion of jurisdiction strictly — any doubt is resolved in favour of jurisdiction.
📌Examples of expressly barred suits: Consumer disputes (Consumer Court), labour disputes (Labour Court), election disputes (Election Tribunal). Courts can examine whether bar is express or implied.

Every suit shall be instituted in the Court of the lowest grade competent to try it.

📌Always file in the LOWEST competent court. Filing in a higher court when a lower is competent is an irregularity — it can be remedied but ideally avoided.

Institution of Suit and Pleadings

O.VII R.1Plaint — particulars required▶

Every plaint shall contain the following particulars: (a) the name of the Court in which the suit is brought; (b) the name, description and place of residence of the plaintiff; (c) the name, description and place of residence of the defendant, so far as they can be ascertained; (d) where the plaintiff or the defendant is a minor or a person of unsound mind, a statement to that effect; (e) the facts constituting the cause of action and when it arose; (f) the facts showing that the Court has jurisdiction; (g) the relief which the plaintiff claims; (h) where the plaintiff has allowed a set-off or relinquished a portion of his claim, the amount so allowed or relinquished; and (i) a statement of the value of the subject-matter of the suit for the purposes of jurisdiction and of court fees, so far as the case admits of such valuation.

📌A plaint is the founding document of a civil suit. Any of these required particulars, if missing, can make the plaint defective — court may return it for correction (O.VII R.9) or in extreme cases reject it (O.VII R.11).
O.VII R.11Rejection of plaint▶

The plaint shall be rejected in the following cases — (a) where it does not disclose a cause of action; (b) where the relief claimed is undervalued, and the plaintiff, on being required by the Court to correct the valuation within a time to be fixed by the Court, fails to do so; (c) where the relief claimed is properly valued, but the plaint is written upon paper insufficiently stamped, and the plaintiff, on being required by the Court to supply the requisite stamp paper within a time to be fixed by the Court, fails to do so; (d) where the suit appears from the statement in the plaint to be barred by any law.

📌Rejection vs Return: REJECTION (O.VII R.11) = plaint thrown out entirely — suit cannot be refiled unless defect is curable. RETURN (O.VII R.10) = plaint given back to plaintiff to file in proper court. Rejection is final; return allows refiling elsewhere.
O.VIIIWritten Statement▶

The defendant must file a written statement within 30 days of summons (or extended time). O.VIII R.1A: Defendant must specifically deny allegations he disputes. A plaint allegation not specifically denied is DEEMED ADMITTED. O.VIII R.6: Set-off — defendant can claim money owed by plaintiff as a set-off in the same suit.

Specific denial rule: If a defendant does not specifically deny a material fact in the written statement, it is deemed admitted. Courts require specific traversal. A general denial 'denies all allegations' is NOT sufficient.
📌Written statement must be filed within 30 days. Court can extend to 90 days. After 90 days, no written statement can be accepted except with showing of good cause.

Res Judicata (§ 11) — The Most Important CPC Principle

§ 11Res Judicata — once decided, not again▶

No Court shall try any suit or issue in which the matter directly and substantially in issue has been directly and substantially in issue in a former suit between the same parties, or between parties under whom they or any of them claim, litigating under the same title, in a Court competent to try such subsequent suit or the suit in which such issue has been subsequently raised, and has been heard and finally decided by such Court.

6 conditions for res judicata: (1) Matter directly and substantially in issue in BOTH suits, (2) SAME parties or parties claiming under them, (3) Litigating under SAME title, (4) Former court was competent to try the present suit, (5) Matter was heard and FINALLY decided, (6) Decided by a court of competent jurisdiction.
Illustration: A sues B for rent for 1420 BS. Court dismisses. A then sues B for the SAME rent for 1420 BS — second suit is barred by res judicata. BUT: A suing for rent for 1421 BS is a fresh cause of action — NOT barred.
Case / Principle: Policy behind res judicata: Interest republicae ut sit finis litium — it is in the interest of the State that there be an end to litigation. Also prevents contradictory judgments on the same issue.
📌Res judicata vs estoppel: Res judicata bars the SUIT; estoppel (§115 Evidence Act) bars the PARTY from making inconsistent assertions. Res judicata is a procedural bar; estoppel is an evidential rule.

Key Orders to Know

Order 39Temporary Injunctions▶

Order 39 Rule 1: Where any property in dispute in a suit is in danger of being wasted, damaged or alienated by any party to the suit, or wrongfully sold in execution of a decree, the Court may grant a temporary injunction to restrain such act, or make such other order for the purpose of staying and preventing the wasting, damaging, alienation, sale, removal or disposition of the property, as the Court thinks fit.

📌Receiver is an officer of the court — manages disputed property during litigation. Avoids deterioration/misappropriation. The court controls what powers the receiver has.

Art. 33Safeguards as to arrest and detention — full text▶

No person who is arrested shall be detained in custody without being informed, as soon as may be, of the grounds for such arrest, nor shall he be denied the right to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner of his choice. Every person who is arrested and detained in custody shall be produced before the nearest magistrate within a period of twenty-four hours of such arrest excluding the time necessary for the journey from the place of arrest to the court of the magistrate and no such person shall be detained in custody beyond the said period without the authority of a magistrate.

Article 33 rights of arrested person: (1) Informed of grounds of arrest (as soon as possible), (2) Right to consult and be defended by a lawyer of choice, (3) Produced before nearest Magistrate within 24 hours (travel time excluded), (4) No detention beyond 24 hours without Magistrate's authority. Violation = habeas corpus (Article 102).
📌Exceptions to Article 33: Enemy aliens and persons detained under preventive detention laws (Article 33(2)). Preventive detention without trial is permitted under special laws — but Article 33(4) and (5) provide procedural safeguards even for preventive detainees.
Art. 35Protection in respect of trial and punishment▶

(1) No person shall be convicted of any offence except for violation of a law in force at the time of the commission of the act charged as an offence, nor be subjected to a penalty greater than, or different from, that which might have been inflicted under the law in force at the time of the commission of the offence. (2) No person shall be prosecuted and punished for the same offence more than once. (3) Every person accused of a criminal offence shall have the right to a speedy and public trial by an independent and impartial Court or tribunal established by law. (4) No person accused of any offence shall be compelled to be a witness against himself. (5) No person shall be subjected to torture or to cruel, inhuman or degrading punishment or treatment.

📌Five protections under Article 35: (1) No ex post facto law, (2) No double jeopardy (autrefois acquit/convict), (3) Speedy and public trial by independent tribunal, (4) No self-incrimination — accused cannot be compelled to testify against himself (corresponds to §342 CrPC), (5) No torture. These all correspond to provisions in CrPC and international human rights law.

The Parliament (Jatiya Sangsad) — Articles 65–93

Art. 65Establishment of Parliament▶

There shall be a Parliament for Bangladesh (to be known as the House of the Nation or Jatiya Sangsad) in which, subject to the provisions of this Constitution, shall be vested the legislative powers of the Republic; and which shall consist of three hundred members to be elected in accordance with law from single territorial constituencies by direct election and, for as long as clause (3) is effective, the members referred to in clause (3).

Article 65(3): 50 seats reserved for women members, to be distributed among parties in proportion to their seats in parliament. These 50 women members are elected indirectly by the directly elected members. Total Parliament = 300 + 50 = 350 members.
📌Article 65 facts for BJS: (1) Unicameral legislature (one house), (2) 300 directly elected + 50 reserved for women = 350 total, (3) Single territorial constituencies, (4) Term = 5 years from first sitting after general election.
Art. 66Qualifications for election to Parliament▶

A person shall, subject to the provisions of clause (2), be qualified to be elected as, and to be, a member of Parliament, if he is a citizen of Bangladesh and if he has attained the age of twenty-five years. (2) A person shall be disqualified for election as, or for being, a member of Parliament if he is declared to be of unsound mind by a competent court; or if he is an undischarged insolvent; or if he acquires citizenship of, or affirms or acknowledges allegiance to, a foreign state; or if he has been, on conviction for a criminal offence involving moral turpitude, sentenced to imprisonment for a term of not less than two years...

📌Qualifications to be an MP: (1) Bangladeshi citizen, (2) At least 25 years old. Disqualifications include: unsound mind (court declared), undischarged insolvent, foreign citizenship/allegiance, convicted of offence of moral turpitude with 2+ years imprisonment, certain govt service connections.
Art. 70Vacation of seat on resignation or voting against party▶

A person elected as a member of Parliament at an election at which he was nominated as a candidate by a political party shall vacate his seat if he resigns from that party after his election, or if he votes in Parliament against that party, or abstains from voting in Parliament in defiance of a direction issued by that party.

📌Article 70 (Floor crossing ban): An MP who was elected on a party ticket CANNOT: (1) resign from the party, (2) vote against the party, (3) abstain from voting against party direction — or he vacates his seat. This provision is unique to Bangladesh and is intended to prevent floor-crossing/defection.

The Judiciary — Articles 94–116

The Supreme Court of Bangladesh consists of two divisions: the High Court Division (HCD) and the Appellate Division (AD). The HCD is the most significant court for practicing lawyers — it has writ jurisdiction to enforce fundamental rights.

Art. 94Establishment of Supreme Court▶

There shall be a Supreme Court for Bangladesh (to be known as the Supreme Court of Bangladesh) comprising the Appellate Division and the High Court Division.

📌Bangladesh has ONE Supreme Court with TWO divisions. There is no separate High Court — the High Court Division IS part of the Supreme Court. The Appellate Division hears appeals from the High Court Division.
Art. 95Appointment of judges▶

The Chief Justice shall be appointed by the President, and the other judges shall be appointed by the President in consultation with the Chief Justice.

📌Appointment: (1) Chief Justice — appointed by President directly. (2) Other judges of SC — appointed by President after consulting the Chief Justice. The consultation is mandatory but advisory — President is not bound to follow CJ's recommendation (though in practice there is convention).
Art. 96Tenure of judges▶

Subject to the other provisions of this article, a Judge shall hold office until he attains the age of sixty-seven years. A Judge shall not be removed from his office except by an order of the President and on the ground of proved misbehaviour or incapacity, and in accordance with the provisions of clause (6).

Judges' tenure: Retire at 67 years. Cannot be removed except by Supreme Judicial Council (SJC) on grounds of proved misbehaviour or incapacity. The SJC consists of the Chief Justice and the two next most senior judges. This protects judicial independence.
📌The Supreme Judicial Council (SJC) recommends removal — the President then removes. This two-step process protects judges from arbitrary removal by the executive.
Art. 101Jurisdiction of High Court Division▶

Subject to the provisions of the Constitution, the High Court Division shall have such original, appellate and other jurisdiction and powers as are or may be conferred on it by the Constitution or any other law.

📌HCD has THREE types of jurisdiction: (1) Original jurisdiction (direct filing), (2) Appellate jurisdiction (hearing appeals from lower courts), (3) Special jurisdiction (writ jurisdiction under Article 102).
Art. 102Powers of High Court Division — WRIT JURISDICTION▶

The High Court Division shall have power, on the application of any aggrieved person, to give such directions or orders to any person or authority, including any person performing any function in connection with the affairs of the Republic, as may be appropriate for the enforcement of any of the fundamental rights conferred by Part III of this Constitution. The High Court Division shall have power to make an order: (a) directing a person in custody to be brought before it so that it may satisfy itself that he is not being held in custody without lawful authority or in an unlawful manner; (b) requiring a person holding or purporting to hold a public office to show under what authority he claims to hold that office; (c) requiring a person or authority to do any act which it is legally required to do; (d) requiring a person or authority to refrain from doing any act which it is not legally authorised to do; (e) setting aside or quashing an order of a court or authority; requiring a court or authority to state a case for the opinion of the High Court Division.

Five types of writs under Article 102:
(1) Habeas Corpus — Clause (a): Bring a person before court to examine legality of detention. 'You shall have the body.' Used when police/authority holds person illegally.
(2) Quo Warranto — Clause (b): By what authority does a person claim to hold public office? Challenges illegal occupancy of public office.
(3) Mandamus — Clause (c): Command to do something legally required. Against public authority failing to perform a statutory duty.
(4) Prohibition — Clause (d): Order to stop doing something not legally authorised. Prevents inferior court/authority from exceeding jurisdiction (issued BEFORE it acts).
(5) Certiorari — Clause (e): Quash an order of inferior court/authority that has exceeded jurisdiction or acted contrary to law (issued AFTER it acts). 'To be certified.'
📌Key distinction — Prohibition vs Certiorari: Prohibition is issued BEFORE the inferior court proceeds further (to stop it). Certiorari is issued AFTER a decision has been made (to quash it). Both deal with excess of jurisdiction but at different stages.
Case / Principle: Mandamus cannot be issued: against the President or PM personally; against courts for judicial decisions; where an alternative remedy exists; where duty is discretionary (not mandatory). Habeas corpus is the most frequently sought writ in criminal matters.
Art. 103Jurisdiction of Appellate Division▶

The Appellate Division shall have jurisdiction to hear and determine appeals from judgments, decrees, orders or sentences of the High Court Division.

📌Appellate Division is the highest court of Bangladesh. Its decisions are binding on ALL courts including the High Court Division (Article 111). Cases reach the AD either by appeal or by the AD granting leave to appeal from HCD decisions.
Art. 111Law declared by Supreme Court is binding▶

The law declared by the Appellate Division shall be binding on the High Court Division and the law declared by either division of the Supreme Court shall be binding on all other courts.

📌Doctrine of precedent (stare decisis): (1) Appellate Division decisions bind the HCD and all lower courts. (2) HCD decisions bind all lower courts but not itself (though usually followed for consistency). (3) Lower court decisions do not bind anyone.

Essentials of a Valid Contract (Section 10)

The Contract Act 1872 governs all contracts in Bangladesh. Section 10 is the definitional foundation: All agreements are contracts if they are made by the free consent of parties competent to contract, for a lawful consideration and with a lawful object, and are not hereby expressly declared to be void.

  • §2(a)
    Offer/Proposal: When one person signifies to another his willingness to do or to abstain from doing anything, with a view to obtaining the assent of that other to such act or abstinence, he is said to make a proposal.
  • §2(b)
    Acceptance: When the person to whom the proposal is made signifies his assent thereto, the proposal is said to be accepted.
  • §2(e)
    Agreement: Every promise and every set of mutual promises forming the consideration for each other. Agreement = Offer + Acceptance.
  • §2(h)
    Contract: An agreement enforceable by law. Contract = Agreement + Enforceability.
  • §10
    Valid contract requires: Free consent + Competent parties + Lawful consideration + Lawful object + Not expressly declared void.
  • §11
    Competence: Major (18+), sound mind, not disqualified by law. A minor's agreement is void ab initio (not even voidable) under Bangladesh law.
  • §2(d)
    Consideration: When at the desire of the promisor, the promisee or any other person has done or abstained from doing, or does or abstains from doing, or promises to do or abstain from doing, something — such act, abstinence or promise is called the consideration for the promise.
§ 11–12Competence to contract — minor's contract▶

§11: Every person is competent to contract who is of the age of majority according to the law to which he is subject, and who is of sound mind, and is not disqualified from contracting by any law to which he is subject. §12: A person is said to be of sound mind for the purpose of making a contract, if, at the time when he makes it, he is capable of understanding it and of forming a rational judgment as to its effect upon his interests.

Minor's contract in Bangladesh: A contract by a minor is VOID AB INITIO — not voidable. This means it has no legal effect from the beginning. The minor cannot be held liable on it. However, a minor can be the BENEFICIARY of a contract (receive benefits without being bound). No estoppel can be raised against a minor for misrepresenting his age.
📌Person of unsound mind: A person usually of unsound mind CAN make a contract during a lucid interval. A person usually of sound mind who is temporarily of unsound mind CANNOT make a valid contract at that time. Each case depends on the mental state at the time of contract.

Performance, Breach and Remedies

§ 37–52Performance of contracts▶

§37: The parties to a contract must either perform, or offer to perform, their respective promises, unless such performance is dispensed with or excused under the provisions of this Act, or of any other law. §38: Where a promisor has made an offer of performance to the promisee, and the offer has not been accepted, the promisor is not responsible for non-performance, nor does he thereby lose his rights under the contract.

📌Performance can be by the promisor himself, his agent, or his legal representative (in case of death). Some contracts require personal performance (e.g., painting a portrait). Tender of performance = offer to perform. If promisee refuses a valid tender, promisor is discharged from obligation.
§ 73Compensation for loss or damage caused by breach▶

When a contract has been broken, the party who suffers by such breach is entitled to receive, from the party who has broken it, compensation for any loss or damage caused to him thereby, which naturally arose in the usual course of things from such breach, or which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from breach of it.

Two heads of damages (Hadley v Baxendale rule adopted): (1) Ordinary/general damages — arising naturally in the usual course from breach; (2) Special damages — consequential losses that both parties knew would likely result. No remote or speculative damages.
📌§73 also: Obligation to mitigate — the aggrieved party MUST take reasonable steps to minimise loss. If they fail to mitigate, they cannot recover for the additional loss that could have been avoided.
§ 74Compensation for breach of contract where penalty is stipulated▶

When a contract has been broken, if a sum is named in the contract as the amount to be paid in case of such breach, or if the contract contains any other stipulation by way of penalty, the party complaining of the breach is entitled, whether or not actual damage or loss is proved to have been caused thereby, to receive from the party who has broken the contract reasonable compensation not exceeding the amount so named or, as the case may be, the penalty stipulated for.

§74 vs English law: In English law, a distinction is made between a genuine pre-estimate of loss (liquidated damages — enforceable) and a penalty (not enforceable). Under §74, Bangladesh courts can award 'reasonable compensation not exceeding the stipulated amount' regardless of whether it was a genuine pre-estimate or penalty. The contract provision is a ceiling, not a floor.
📌§74 — no need to prove actual loss. Even if no actual damage, the court can award reasonable compensation up to the stipulated amount. But the amount is a cap — court will not award more even if actual loss exceeds it.
§ 75Party rightfully rescinding contract entitled to compensation▶

A person who rightfully rescinds a contract is entitled to compensation for any damage which he has sustained through the non-fulfilment of the contract.

📌§75: Rescission + damages. If A rightfully rescinds a contract because B breached it, A can claim damages for the losses suffered due to B's non-fulfilment. Rescission and damages can both be claimed.

Sale of Immoveable Property (§§ 54–57)

§ 54Sale — definition▶

'Sale' is a transfer of ownership in exchange for a price paid or promised or part-paid and part-promised. Such transfer, in the case of tangible immoveable property of the value of one hundred taka and upwards, or in the case of a reversion or other intangible thing, can be made only by a registered instrument. In the case of tangible immoveable property of a value less than one hundred taka, such transfer may be made either by a registered instrument or by delivery of the property.

Essentials of sale: (1) Transfer of ownership (title passes), (2) In exchange for price (money), (3) Tangible immoveable property ≥ Tk.100: MUST be by registered instrument. Without registration, no valid sale — even if possession is given and price paid.
📌Sale vs Gift: Sale requires price (consideration). Gift (§122) is without consideration. Sale vs Mortgage: Sale transfers ownership; mortgage transfers only an interest as security. Sale vs Lease: Sale transfers ownership; lease transfers only right to enjoy (possession).

Lease, Exchange, and Gift

§ 105Lease — definition▶

A lease of immoveable property is a transfer of a right to enjoy such property, made for a certain time, express or implied, or in perpetuity, in consideration of a price paid or promised, or of money, a share of crops, service or any other thing of value, to be rendered periodically or on specified occasions to the transferor by the transferee, who accepts the transfer on such terms.

Essentials of lease: (1) Transfer of RIGHT TO ENJOY (not ownership), (2) Immoveable property, (3) For a certain time or perpetuity, (4) In consideration of price/rent, (5) Acceptance by lessee. The lessor retains ownership; lessee gets only possession/enjoyment.
📌Sale vs Lease: In SALE, ownership transfers — buyer becomes owner. In LEASE, only right to enjoy transfers — lessee is possessor, lessor remains owner. This is the fundamental distinction.
§ 107Leases how made▶

A lease of immoveable property from year to year, or for any term exceeding one year, or reserving a yearly rent, can be made only by a registered instrument. All other leases of immoveable property may be made either by a registered instrument or by oral agreement accompanied by delivery of possession.

📌Lease registration rule: (1) Lease from year to year, OR more than 1 year, OR yearly rent reserved: MUST be registered. (2) Monthly lease, or any lease not exceeding 1 year: Can be oral if accompanied by possession.

Specific Performance of Contracts

Specific performance is a court order compelling a party to perform his contractual obligations. It is an equitable remedy — the court has discretion whether to grant it. It is usually granted where money compensation would be inadequate.

§ 12When specific performance may be enforced▶

Except as otherwise provided in this Act, specific performance of a contract may, in the discretion of the Court, be enforced in the following cases, namely: (a) when the act agreed to be done is in the performance, wholly or partly, of a trust; (b) when there exists no standard for ascertaining the actual damage caused by non-performance of the act agreed to be done; (c) when the act agreed to be done is such that pecuniary compensation for its non-performance would not afford adequate relief; (d) when it is probable that pecuniary compensation cannot be got for the non-performance of the act agreed to be done.

When to grant specific performance: (1) Performance of a trust, (2) No standard to ascertain damages, (3) Monetary compensation inadequate, (4) Monetary compensation cannot be obtained. Contracts for immoveable property typically qualify because land is unique — money cannot fully substitute for the specific land contracted for.
📌Specific performance is discretionary — even if a plaintiff technically qualifies, the court may refuse if: plaintiff has not performed his obligations; plaintiff has delayed unconscionably; plaintiff comes with unclean hands; enforcement would be oppressive.
§ 21When specific performance NOT enforced▶

Specific performance cannot be enforced in favour of — (a) a person who would not be entitled to recover compensation for its breach; (b) a company, the memorandum of association of which does not authorise it to enter into such a contract; (c) a person where the contract involves the performance of a continuous duty which the Court cannot supervise; (d) a person who has become incapable of performing, or violates any essential term of, the contract before application for performance is made; (e) a person whose contract is inherently uncertain.

📌Personal service contracts: NEVER specifically enforced. You cannot get a court order forcing a singer to perform, a player to play, or a painter to paint — courts cannot supervise such ongoing personal services. The remedy is damages only.

§ 3Bar of limitation — fundamental rule▶

Subject to the provisions contained in sections 4 to 25 (inclusive), every suit instituted, appeal preferred, and application made after the period of limitation prescribed therefor by the first schedule shall be dismissed, although limitation has not been set up as a defence.

📌Limitation is a bar to the remedy, not to the right. The right still exists even if the suit is time-barred — but the court will not enforce it. Importantly, the court must dismiss a time-barred suit EVEN IF the defendant does not plead limitation. The court can raise it suo motu.

Exclusion, Condonation, and Extension of Time

§ 4Expiry on holiday — suit filed on next day▶

Where the period of limitation prescribed for any suit, appeal or application expires on a day when the Court is closed, the suit, appeal or application may be instituted, preferred or made on the day when the Court reopens.

📌If the last day of limitation is a Sunday or holiday, the suit can be filed on the next working day. This is NOT an extension of limitation — it is only a rule about closed court days.
§ 5Extension for sufficient cause — ONLY for appeals and applications▶

Any appeal or any application, other than an application under any of the provisions of Order XXI of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, may be admitted after the prescribed period if the appellant or applicant satisfies the Court that he had sufficient cause for not preferring the appeal or making the application within such period.

Section 5 — Critical rules: (1) Applies ONLY to appeals and applications — NOT to original suits. (2) The court has discretion — may condone the delay if 'sufficient cause' shown. (3) 'Sufficient cause' = reason beyond the control of the party; legal advice to not file; illness; natural disaster etc. (4) Sufficient cause must cover the ENTIRE period of delay.
📌Most tested MCQ: Does §5 apply to original suits? NO. A suit filed after limitation cannot be condoned under §5. There is no provision in the Limitation Act to excuse delay in filing a suit (unlike appeals/applications). A time-barred suit must be dismissed.
§ 12Exclusion of time in legal proceedings▶

In computing the period of limitation for any suit, appeal or application, the day from which such period is to be reckoned shall be excluded; and, where the prescribed period for any suit, appeal or application is a month or months and a half-month from a specified day, the period shall be held to terminate on the same day of the corresponding calendar month.

📌The day from which limitation runs (the cause of action date) is EXCLUDED in calculating the period. So if cause of action accrues on 1 January and limitation is 3 years, the period runs until 1 January (3 years later) but the 1st January itself is excluded from counting.
§ 14Exclusion of time when proceeding bona fide in wrong court▶

In computing the period of limitation for any suit, the time during which the plaintiff has been prosecuting with due diligence another civil proceeding, whether in a court of first instance or of appeal, against the defendant, shall be excluded, where such proceeding is founded upon the same cause of action and is prosecuted in good faith in a court which, from defect of jurisdiction or other cause of a like nature, is unable to entertain it.

📌§14 helps plaintiffs who filed in the wrong court in good faith. The time spent in the wrong court is excluded when calculating limitation for the correct court. Requirements: (1) Prosecution with due diligence, (2) Same cause of action, (3) Good faith, (4) Court was unable to entertain it (not merely that judgment was against plaintiff).
§ 16Effect of death on limitation▶

Where the person against whom or in favour of whom a right to sue accrues dies before the period of limitation has expired, or where such right accrues on the death of a person, the period of limitation shall be computed from the date of the death of such person, or where the cause of action survives or is against the estate of the deceased, from the date of the death.

📌If the plaintiff or defendant dies, a fresh period of limitation starts from the date of death (for the legal representative to sue or be sued). This prevents unfairness where a party dies close to the limitation deadline.

Muslim Marriage (Nikah)

Muslim marriage (Nikah) is a civil contract — not a sacrament. This is its fundamental nature under Islamic law. It requires an offer, acceptance, presence of witnesses, and dower (Mahr).

  • 1
    Proposal (Ijab): Made by one party in the presence of both parties at the same meeting (majlis). Must be unambiguous.
  • 2
    Acceptance (Qabul): Must be immediate — in the same majlis. Cannot be conditional.
  • 3
    Witnesses: Two adult Muslim male witnesses OR one male and two female Muslim witnesses. Their presence is essential for validity.
  • 4
    Dower (Mahr): A sum of money or property payable by husband to wife as a mark of respect. Can be prompt (mu'ajjal — payable immediately) or deferred (mu'wajjal — payable on death or divorce). Absence of specified mahr — proper mahr (mahr-i-misl) is implied.
  • 5
    Capacity: Both parties must have legal capacity — adult (18 for males, 16 for females under CMRA 2017) and of sound mind. Below these ages — consent of guardian required under Islamic law though CMRA 2017 prohibits child marriage.
  • 6
    No prohibited degrees: Cannot marry certain close relatives (consanguinity, affinity, fosterage bars apply).
Muslim Marriages Registration Act 1974Compulsory registration▶

The Muslim Marriages and Divorces (Registration) Act 1974 makes registration of Muslim marriages compulsory in Bangladesh. A Kazi (registrar) registers the marriage in the Nikah Register. Failure to register is an offence punishable with fine. However, non-registration does NOT invalidate the marriage — an unregistered Nikah is still valid but the parties are liable for the offence of non-registration.

📌Registration provides evidence of marriage — important in property, custody, and divorce disputes. Always advise clients to register their marriages.

Bangladesh Bar Council — Structure and Functions

OverviewEstablishment and composition▶

The Bangladesh Bar Council was established under the Legal Practitioners and Bar Council Order 1972 (P.O. No. 46 of 1972). It is a statutory body that regulates the legal profession in Bangladesh.

Composition of Bar Council: (1) Attorney-General of Bangladesh (ex-officio Chairman), (2) Advocates elected from each Division/District, (3) Members elected by advocates on the Supreme Court roll. Total membership varies. Quorum: as specified in Rules.
📌The Bar Council is NOT a government body — it is a self-regulatory body of the legal profession. Its primary purpose is to maintain standards, protect the public, and regulate the profession.

Enrollment as an Advocate

QualificationsRequirements for enrollment▶

To be enrolled as an advocate on the roll of Bangladesh Bar Council, an applicant must: (1) be a citizen of Bangladesh; (2) have obtained a law degree (LLB or equivalent) from a recognized university; (3) pass the Bar Council Enrollment Examination; (4) not be of unsound mind; (5) not have been convicted of an offence involving moral turpitude; (6) not have been removed from service for misconduct; (7) pay the prescribed enrollment fee.

📌After enrollment, you are an 'Advocate' entitled to practice in all subordinate courts throughout Bangladesh.

Bangladesh · Criminal Procedure
Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898 (CrPC)
Governs investigation, trial, and sentencing of criminal offences. 565 sections. Works alongside the Penal Code — the Penal Code defines crimes, CrPC defines how they are tried.
565 Sections🔴 BJS Highest PriorityWorks with Penal Code

Courts and Their Classification

The CrPC establishes a hierarchy of criminal courts. Each court has jurisdiction over specific offences based on the maximum punishment that can be awarded.

CourtEstablished byMaximum Sentence it can impose
Chief Metropolitan Magistrate / Chief Judicial Magistrate§§ 6, 125 years imprisonment + fine
Metropolitan Magistrate / Judicial Magistrate (1st Class)§ 125 years imprisonment + fine
Judicial Magistrate (2nd Class)§ 123 years imprisonment + fine up to Tk.3000
Judicial Magistrate (3rd Class)§ 122 years imprisonment + fine up to Tk.1000
Sessions Court§ 6Any sentence authorised by law (including death — but needs HC confirmation)
Additional Sessions Judge§ 9Same as Sessions Judge
High Court DivisionConstitution Art.101Any sentence; appellate and original jurisdiction
Death sentence confirmation: Under §374 CrPC, a Sessions Court that passes a death sentence must submit the case to the High Court Division for confirmation. The death sentence does not take effect until the High Court confirms it.
📌 Cognizable vs Non-Cognizable: The most fundamental classification of offences in CrPC. Schedule II classifies every offence. Cognizable = police can arrest without warrant, investigate without Magistrate's order. Non-cognizable = police need Magistrate's permission for investigation and arrest.

Arrest by policeWithout warrant (§54)Only with warrant
InvestigationWithout Magistrate's order (§156)Only with Magistrate's order (§155)
ExamplesMurder, rape, robbery, dacoity, kidnappingAssault, cheating (some), defamation, public nuisance

FIR and Investigation (§§ 154–176)

§ 154First Information Report (FIR) — Cognizable offences▶

Every information relating to the commission of a cognizable offence, if given orally to an officer in charge of a police station, shall be reduced to writing by him or under his direction, and be read over to the informant; and every such information, whether given in writing or reduced to writing as aforesaid, shall be signed by the person giving it, and the substance thereof shall be entered in a book kept for that purpose by such officer in the form prescribed by the Government, and a copy of the information as so recorded shall be given forthwith, free of cost, to the informant.

Essentials of a valid FIR: (1) Must relate to a cognizable offence, (2) If oral, must be reduced to writing by the officer, (3) Must be read over to the informant, (4) Signed by the informant, (5) Entered in the station diary, (6) A free copy must be given to the informant immediately.
